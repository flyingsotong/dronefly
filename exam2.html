1. Principles of Flight

Aerodynamics, stability, and control systems governing aircraft movement

‹
›
Question 1
What are the four fundamental forces acting on an aircraft during flight?
Lift, Weight, Thrust, and Drag
Lift: Upward force created by wings or propellers
Weight: Downward force due to gravity
Thrust: Force pulling aircraft forward, created by propellers or engines
Drag: Force opposing thrust, caused by air resistance and aircraft shape

Flight Conditions:
• To ascend: Lift > Weight
• To maintain altitude: Lift = Weight
• To descend: Lift < Weight
• To increase speed: Thrust > Drag
• To maintain constant speed: Thrust = Drag
• To reduce speed: Thrust < Drag
Question 2
When does an aerodynamic stall occur?
When the critical angle of attack is exceeded
Airfoil: Body shaped to produce aerodynamic reaction (lift) perpendicular to motion
Bernoulli's Principle: Higher air speed = reduced pressure, lower air speed = increased pressure
Angle of Attack (AOA): Angle between wing chord line and oncoming wind
Critical AOA: Maximum lift achieved; exceeding causes stall
Aerodynamic Stall: Occurs when UA cannot produce enough lift to support weight
Question 3
What are the two main types of aircraft stability?
Static and Dynamic Stability
Static Stability:
• Positive: Tendency to return to original state
• Neutral: Tendency to remain at new state
• Negative: Tendency to diverge further

Dynamic Stability:
• Positive: Dampens oscillations toward original state
• Neutral: Maintains oscillation magnitude
• Negative: Diverges with increasing oscillations

Aircraft must be both statically and dynamically stable
Question 4
What happens when an aircraft's center of gravity moves forward?
Stability increases and pitch maneuverability decreases
Forward CG:
• Increases longitudinal stability
• Decreases pitch maneuverability
• Higher stall speed
• Longer take-off distance

Aft CG:
• Decreases longitudinal stability
• Increases pitch maneuverability
• Lower stall speed
• Poor stall/spin recovery
Question 5
Which control surface is responsible for changes in pitch?
Elevator
Elevator: Controls pitch (nose up/down) around lateral axis
Ailerons: Control roll (wing tilting) around longitudinal axis
Rudder: Controls yaw (nose left/right) around vertical axis

Multi-Rotor Controls: Pitch, roll, and yaw controlled by varying motor speeds
Helicopter Controls: Swashplate for pitch/roll, tail motor AOA for yaw
Question 6
At what distance from the ground does ground effect occur?
Within ½ wingspan of the ground
Ground Effect: Occurs when wing or multi-rotor is less than ½ wingspan above ground

Effects:
• Reduces induced drag
• Increases lift coefficient
• May allow premature lift-off
• Can cause controllability issues

Vortex Ring State (VRS): Unwanted aerodynamic effect affecting helicopters and multi-rotors

2. Unmanned Aircraft Systems

UAS components, flight control systems, and operational procedures

‹
›
Question 7
What is the difference between a UA and a UAS?
UA is the aircraft, UAS includes the aircraft and associated elements
Unmanned Aircraft (UA): Aircraft flown without individuals on board
Unmanned Aircraft System (UAS): UA and associated elements

UA Categories:
• Aeroplane: Fixed-wing, higher endurance than rotorcraft
• Rotorcraft: Multi-rotors, helicopters
• Powered-lift: VTOL capable, engine-driven lift devices
• Airship: Lighter-than-air, prolonged surveillance capability

Applications: Military, inspection, agriculture, photography, surveying, surveillance, law enforcement, search and rescue
Question 8
What is the maximum voltage of a fully charged 6S LiPo battery?
25.2V
LiPo Batteries:
• Lighter with high energy density
• Safest charge rate: 1C (1x capacity in Amps)
• 6S LiPo at 100% charge: 25.2V maximum
• Charge with approved equipment, operator present

Power Distribution Board (PDB): Connects and distributes power from battery to all electrical components and ESCs
Question 10
What are the main components of a UAS propulsion system?
ESCs, Motors, Propellers
Electronic Speed Controllers (ESCs):
• Interpret flight controller inputs
• Convert to pulses for motor speed control
• Must be paired with compatible motor

Motors: Brushless DC motors, adjacent motors spin opposite directions

Propellers: Convert spinning motion to controlled forward motion
Question 9
What is the minimum number of satellites needed for GPS positioning?
4 satellites
Flight Controller (FC): Onboard computer for piloting UA

Sensors:
• Accelerometer: Measures acceleration, detects vibrations
• Gyroscope: Measures orientation
• Magnetic Compass: Measures direction
• Barometer: Measures atmospheric pressure

GPS: Minimum 4 satellites needed for position calculation
Question 11
What are the two main components of a radio control system?
RC Transmitter and Receiver
Remote Control Transmitter (RC Tx):
• Operates at 2.4GHz frequency
• Sends basic flight commands (throttle, elevator, aileron, rudder)

Radio Receiver (Rx): Receives and translates signals to flight controller

Binding: Links specific receiver to specific transmitter

Range: Line of Sight for 2.4GHz radio

Channels: Multi-rotor UA requires minimum 4 channels
Question 12
What is the most difficult flight control mode for UA operation?
Manual/Rates Mode
Manual/Rates Mode: Most difficult, no control augmentation
Attitude Hold: Keeps UA level when sticks centered
Altitude Hold: Maintains constant altitude/height
GPS Mode: Position hold, autonomous flight, RTH functions
Return-to-Home (RTH): Climbs to predetermined altitude, flies to home point
Collision Avoidance: UA avoids obstacles
Headless Mode: Control without considering UA orientation
Guidance Systems
Advanced navigation and sensing technologies
RTK, Infrared, Vision, Ultrasound, LiDAR
Real Time Kinematic (RTK): High-performance locating near base station
Infrared Sensor: Detects obstacles without visible light
Vision-based System: Forward-facing cameras for collision avoidance
Ultrasound System: High-frequency sound pulses for ground detection
LiDAR System: Pulsed laser beams for long-distance sensing
UAS Operations
Operational phases and procedures
Pre-flight, In-flight, Post-flight
Pre-flight: Environment evaluation, control link checks, power levels, structural checks, calibration
In-flight: Basic flight control checks, concentrate on operations
Post-flight: Disarm UA, visual inspection, document flight logs

Emergency Procedures:
• Loss of control link: Move closer, adjust antenna, RTH after 8 seconds
• Loss of power: RTH at 10% battery
• Loss of GPS: Fly manually, avoid obstacles
• Loss of orientation: Ensure GPS mode, hover, confirm orientation

3. Human Factors

Psychology, physiology, and decision-making in aviation operations

‹
›
Question 13
What does the SHELL model analyze in aviation systems?
The interaction between Software, Hardware, Environment, and Liveware
SHELL Model: Analyzes interaction between system components

Software: Rules, methods, documentation, standard operating procedures
Hardware: Physical components like ATC suites, controls, displays, systems
Environment: Operating conditions including social, economic, and natural surroundings
Liveware: Human beings within the system (controllers, flight crews, engineers, management)

Human error often caused by mismatch between human factors and other components
Question 15
What is autokinesis in aviation?
A fixed light appears to move in a dark environment
Vision Illusions:
• Autokinesis: Fixed light appears to move in dark environment
• Disorientation: Can occur when UA flies further away, in foggy weather, with extended landing gear, or near buildings
• Visual Scanning: Short, regularly spaced eye movements

Sun-blindness (Photokeratitis): Painful eye condition from UV exposure

Medication: Must be physically and mentally fit; avoid medications causing drowsiness, lethargy, nausea, hand tremors, dizziness, or delayed response
Question 14
How long must a UA pilot wait after consuming alcohol before flying?
At least 8 hours
8-Hour Rule: UA pilots not permitted to fly after consuming alcohol or under influence of drugs for at least 8 hours

Penalties:
• First Offence: Fine not exceeding $50,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 2 years, or both
• Second Offence: Fine not exceeding $100,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 5 years, or both

Psychoactive Substances: Chemicals affecting central nervous system (alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, marijuana)
Question 16
Which hazardous attitude is characterized by "Do it quickly"?
Impulsivity
Fatigue: Overall feeling of tiredness leading to accidents, loss of control, delayed responses

Stress: Can be physical, psychological, or physiological
• Sources: Unfavorable weather, completion bias, "Get-home-itis"
• Management: Identify source, seek support, time management, balanced lifestyle

Hazardous Attitudes:
• Impulsivity: "Do it quickly" - Counter: "Not so fast. Think first."
• Macho: Overestimating ability, underestimating situation
• Anti-authority: "Don't tell me"
• Invulnerability: "It won't happen to me"

4. Navigation & Meteorology

Coordinate systems, weather conditions, and atmospheric properties

‹
›
Navigation
Coordinate systems and navigation technologies
Coordinate Systems and GNSS
Geographic Coordinate System: Set of numbers to specify location on Earth

Longitude: Angle east/west of Prime Meridian (Greenwich)
• Earth rotates 15° per hour
• Every 15° corresponds to distinct time zone

Latitude: Angle between equatorial plane and location to Earth's center
• Equator (0° latitude) separates Northern and Southern Hemispheres

UTM: 60 zones, each 6-degree segment of Earth

GNSS: Constellation of satellites (GPS, GLONASS, BeiDou, Galileo)
• Two common errors: timing and satellite orbit

OneMap: Most accurate fly-zone information (www.OneMap.gov.sg)
Atmospheric Properties
Air density, pressure, temperature, and humidity effects
Air Density and Performance
Air Density: Mass of air per unit volume

Factors Affecting Density:
• Temperature: Warmer air is less dense
• Pressure: Decreases with height
• Humidity: Humid air is less dense

Aircraft Performance:
• Better performance in denser air
• More lift generation possible
• Air is denser under: higher pressure, low temperature, low humidity, low altitude

Density Altitude: Same reading as pressure altitude when standard temperature exists
Altimetry
Altitude measurements and terminology
Altitude Terms and Measurements
Above Ground Level (AGL): Height above the ground
Above Mean Sea Level (AMSL): Height above average sea level datum
At Take-off (ATO): Elevation at take-off, measured above mean sea level

Example Calculation:
UA launched from 20ft, climbs to 30ft = 50ft altitude

Altimeter Operation: Measures decrease in static pressure to determine altitude
Weather Conditions
Clouds, wind types, and weather information
Clouds and Wind Types
Cloud Types:
• Cumulus: Puffy, fluffy looking, resembling cotton balls
• Cumulonimbus: Tower-like with bulging upper regions

Wind Types:
• Headwind: Acts against flight path, aids take-offs and landings
• Tailwind: Acts in direction of flight path, beneficial during cruising
• Crosswind: Perpendicular to line of motion, causes lateral movement

Weather Information:
• Meteorological Services Singapore: weather.gov.sg
• METAR: Weather-reporting format, typically valid for 1 hour
• Okta: Unit of measurement for cloud cover
Weather Reports
METAR format and weather reporting
METAR Information
METAR (METeorological Terminal Aviation Routine):
• Weather-reporting format
• Typically valid for 1 hour

Information Included:
• Wind direction and speed
• Visibility
• Cloud cover (e.g., FEW020TCU, BKN280)
• Temperature/dewpoint
• Atmospheric pressure (QNH)

Cloud Cover Examples:
• FEW020TCU: Few towering cumulus clouds at 2000ft AGL
• BKN280: Broken clouds at 28000ft AGL
Wind Effects
Impact of wind on aircraft performance
Take-off and Landing Effects
Tailwind during Take-off:
• Requires more take-off distance
• Increases ground speed

Headwind during Take-off:
• Lower ground speed
• Shorter runway distance needed
• Creates additional lift

Crosswind:
• Perpendicular to flight path
• Causes lateral movement
• Requires compensation during flight

5. Air Law

Air Navigation Act, permits, licenses, and operational requirements

‹
›
Question 12
What is the penalty for dangerous flying under the Air Navigation Act?
Fine not exceeding $100,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 10 years, or both
Air Navigation Act 1966 (ANA):
• Provides control and regulation of aviation
• Promotes safety and security
• Implements international obligations

Key Provisions:
• Applicability of UAS
• Permits for overflight
• Prohibition of dangerous materials
• Regulations on discharge from UA
• Dangerous activity regulations
• Flying without satisfying safety requirements
• Trespassing at aerodromes
Penalties for Offences
Fines and imprisonment for various violations
Penalty Structure
Dangerous flying / Endangering life or property:
• Fine not exceeding $100,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 10 years, or both

Operating unregistered registrable UA / Tampering with registration labels:
• Fine up to $10,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 6 months, or both

Failure to report accidents:
• 1st offence: Fine not exceeding $50,000
• 2nd offence: Fine not exceeding $100,000

Unauthorised photography over protected areas:
• 1st offence: Fine not exceeding $50,000 or 2 years imprisonment, or both
• 2nd offence: Fine not exceeding $100,000 or 5 years imprisonment, or both
UAS Operations Purposes
Different categories of UA operations
Recreation, Education, Commercial
Recreation Purpose:
• Activities for enjoyment, relaxation, or leisure
• Excludes organized sporting, business-provided recreational activities, or flying displays

Education Purpose:
• Lectures, tutorials, demonstrations, or classes on UA
• Offered by educational institutions to enrolled students

Non-Recreation or Non-Education Purpose (Commercial/Business):
• Subject to more stringent regulations
• Examples: sharing aerial photographs for marketing, educational institutions taking photos for events, volunteers using UA for charity events
Airspace Restrictions
No-fly zones and restricted areas in Singapore
No-Fly Zones and Restricted Areas
OneMap: Useful tool showing No-Fly Zones (www.OneMap.gov.sg)

No-Fly Zones Include:
• Areas within 5km of aerodromes (Changi Airport, Seletar Airport, Tengah Airbase)
• Danger Areas
• Protected Areas under Section 32 of ANA (MINDEF/SAF training camps, police facilities, government buildings, Jurong Island, Tuas Power Station, prisons, checkpoints, offshore islands)
• Prohibited Areas (Central Water Catchment, Cable Car Services from Mt. Faber to Sentosa)
• Restricted Areas
• Temporary Restricted Areas (TRA) established by CAAS
Question 13
What is the weight threshold for UA registration with CAAS?
250 grams
Registration Requirement:
• All UA exceeding 250 grams must be registered with CAAS
• Registrant must be at least 16 years old

Registration Process:
• Purchase registration label with unique UA registration number
• Complete online registration
• Labels available online or at designated SingPost post offices

Purchase Limits:
• Citizens/permanent residents: up to 5 labels
• Others: 1 label
• Each label costs $25

Label Placement:
• Must be affixed on outer surface of non-detachable part of UA
• Must be clearly visible
• Not on removable batteries or propeller blades
Permits and Licenses
Different types of permits and licensing requirements
Operator Permit, Activity Permit, UAPL
Operator Permit:
• Granted by CAAS to organization or individual
• Demonstrates ability to operate UA safely
• Valid for up to one year

Activity Permit (AP):
• Granted for single or block activities
• Considers location, type of operation, date/time, altitude, mitigation measures

Class 1 Activity Permit:
• Required for non-recreational/non-educational purposes
• Or if recreational UA over 25kg, educational UA over 7kg
• Not valid without UA Operator Permit

Class 2 Activity Permit:
• Required for recreational UA (25kg or below) or educational UA (7kg or below)
• When flying above 200 feet AMSL or within No-Fly Zone
Unmanned Aircraft Pilot Licence
UAPL requirements and classifications
Class A and Class B UAPL
UAPL Process:
• Register CAPELS account
• Pass UAPL theory test ($125)
• Attend training (CAAS-approved UATO recommended)
• Pass practical assessment by Authorised Flight Examiner (AFE)
• Submit application ($500 fee)
• Applicant must be at least 16 years old

Class A UAPL:
• Operate UA with total mass not exceeding 25 kg
• Does not cover Military Drones

Class B UAPL:
• Operate UA with total mass exceeding 25 kg

Requirements:
• Pass proficiency check every 4 years
• Class B holders need annual refresher training
• Maintain Visual Line of Sight (VLOS) at all times unless licensed for BVLOS
UA Basic Training Certificate
UABT requirements and supervision
UABT Requirements
UA Basic Training Certificate (UABT):
• Required for operating drone between 1.5kg and 7kg for recreational or educational purposes
• Applicant must be at least 16 years old
• Complete e-learning course and online quiz

Supervision:
• 12-year-old son allowed to operate 20kg UA if supervised by father with relevant UAPL
• 12-year-old son flying 5kg UA for recreational purposes in fly zone within 100ft allowed if supervised by father with relevant UABT

FPV Operations:
• 1kg FPV for fun in outdoor fly-zone requires visual observer

Key Concepts & Study Areas

Principles of Flight

  • Four Forces: Lift, Weight, Thrust, Drag - must be balanced for stable flight
  • Stall Conditions: Occurs when critical angle of attack is exceeded
  • Ground Effect: Occurs within ½ wingspan of ground, reduces drag
  • Center of Gravity: Forward CG = more stability, less maneuverability
  • Control Surfaces: Elevator (pitch), Ailerons (roll), Rudder (yaw)
  • Vortex Ring State: Affects helicopters and multi-rotors during descent

Unmanned Aircraft Systems

  • UA Categories: Aeroplane, Rotorcraft, Powered-lift, Airship
  • LiPo Batteries: 1C charge rate, 6S = 25.2V max, charge with operator present
  • GPS Requirements: Minimum 4 satellites for 3D positioning
  • Radio Control: 2.4GHz Line of Sight, minimum 4 channels for multi-rotor
  • Flight Modes: Manual, Attitude Hold, Altitude Hold, GPS, RTH
  • Guidance Systems: RTK, Infrared, Vision, Ultrasound, LiDAR

Human Factors

  • SHELL Model: Software, Hardware, Environment, Liveware interactions
  • 8-Hour Rule: No flying after alcohol/drugs for at least 8 hours
  • Hazardous Attitudes: Impulsivity, Macho, Anti-authority, Invulnerability
  • Decision Models: DECIDE, PAVE, IMSAFE, 5P, 3P frameworks
  • Vision Illusions: Autokinesis, Disorientation, False horizon
  • Stress Management: Identify source, seek support, balanced lifestyle

Navigation & Meteorology

  • Coordinate Systems: Longitude (15°/hour), Latitude, UTM (60 zones)
  • Air Density: Higher pressure, low temp, low humidity, low altitude = better performance
  • Altitude Terms: AGL (Above Ground), AMSL (Above Mean Sea Level)
  • Wind Effects: Headwind aids take-off/landing, Tailwind requires more distance
  • Weather Reports: METAR format, valid 1 hour, includes cloud cover (Okta)
  • OneMap: Most accurate fly-zone information source

Air Law

  • Registration: Required for UA over 250g, minimum age 16
  • UAPL Classifications: Class A (up to 25kg), Class B (over 25kg)
  • UABT: Required for 1.5kg-7kg recreational/educational operations
  • Activity Permits: Class 1 (commercial), Class 2 (recreational above 200ft)
  • No-Fly Zones: 5km from aerodromes, protected areas, prohibited areas
  • Penalties: Dangerous flying ($100k/10 years), substance influence ($100k/5 years)

Emergency Procedures

  • Loss of Control Link: Move closer, adjust antenna, RTH after 8 seconds
  • Loss of Power: RTH at 10% battery, auto-land if GPS lost
  • Loss of GPS: Fly manually, avoid obstacles, switch to manual mode
  • Loss of Orientation: Ensure GPS mode, hover, confirm orientation
  • Pre-flight: Environment evaluation, control checks, structural inspection
  • Post-flight: Disarm, visual inspection, document flight logs
