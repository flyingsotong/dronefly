Important Concepts & Definitions

Unmanned Aircraft (UA) vs. Unmanned Aircraft System (UAS)

  • UA: An aircraft that may be flown or used without any individual on board to operate it (commonly known as drones).
  • UAS: An unmanned aircraft and its associated elements.

UA Categories

  • Aeroplane: A power-driven, heavier-than-air aircraft, deriving lift primarily from fixed aerodynamic surfaces.
  • Rotorcraft: A power-driven, heavier-than-air aircraft, deriving lift primarily from rotating airfoils (e.g., multi-rotor, helicopter).
  • Powered-lift: A heavier-than-air aircraft capable of vertical take-off and landing (VTOL) and low-speed flight, relying on engine-driven lift devices or thrust for lift, and non-rotating aerofoils for horizontal flight.
  • Airship: A lighter-than-air aircraft capable of vertical take-off/landing and low-speed flight, relying on engine-driven lift devices or thrust for lift, and non-rotating aerofoils for horizontal flight.

Four Forces of Flight

  • Lift: The upward force created by wings or propellers.
  • Weight: The downward force due to gravity.
  • Thrust: The force pulling the aircraft forward, created by a propeller or engine.
  • Drag: The force acting opposite to thrust, created by air resistance.

Angle of Attack

  • The angle between the chord line of the aerofoil and the relative wind direction. Increasing the angle of attack increases both lift and induced drag.

Critical Angle of Attack

  • The angle at which maximum lift force is achieved. Exceeding this angle results in a stall.

Stall

  • Occurs when the UA cannot produce enough lift to support its weight, typically when the critical angle of attack is exceeded or flying below stall speed. Signs include buffeting, poor pitch/roll authority, inability to arrest descent, and nose drop.

Spin

  • A condition following a stall, often caused by one wing stalling, leading to increased drag and decreased lift on that side, causing the UA to drop to the side of the stalled wing and rapidly lose height at a low airspeed.

Ground Effect

  • Occurs when the wing or multi-rotor gets close to the ground (less than one-half the wingspan above the ground), reducing drag due to interaction with the ground, vortices, and downwash. This can allow an aircraft to lift off at lower speeds/power but may cause controllability issues.

Vortex Ring State (VRS)

  • Also known as "settling with power," this is an unwanted aerodynamic effect impacting helicopters and multi-rotors where the airflow around propellers forms rings of flow, including reverse upward airflow. This can lead to controllability issues, rapid descent, instability, and potential crashes.

Static and Dynamic Stability

  • Static Stability: The initial tendency of an aircraft to return to its original flight condition after being disturbed.
    • Positive Static Stability: The aircraft returns to its original state after a disturbance.
    • Neutral Static Stability: The aircraft remains at a new state after a disturbance.
    • Negative Static Stability: The aircraft diverges further from its original state after a disturbance.
  • Dynamic Stability: The tendency of the aircraft's oscillations to dampen and move towards its original state over time.
    • Positive Dynamic Stability: Oscillations dampen and move toward the original state.

Center of Gravity (CG)

  • The specific point where the average mass or weight of an aircraft is centered. The CG must always be within limits, and its location affects longitudinal and lateral stability.
    • Forward CG: Increases longitudinal stability, decreases pitch maneuverability, lowers cruise speed, higher stall speed, longer take-off distance.
    • Aft CG: Decreases longitudinal stability, increases pitch maneuverability, higher cruise speed, lower stall speed, poor stall/spin recovery.

Axes of Motion & Control Surfaces

  • Pitch (rotation around the Lateral Axis): Controlled by the Elevator/Stabilator.
  • Roll (rotation around the Longitudinal Axis): Controlled by the Aileron.
  • Yaw (rotation around the Vertical Axis): Controlled by the Rudder.

LiPo Battery Charging

  • The safest charge rate for most LiPo batteries is 1C, or 1x the capacity of the battery in Amps. Always be present during charging and do not charge overnight. A fully charged LiPo cell is 4.2V.

UA Registration

  • All UAs with a total weight exceeding 250 grams must be registered with CAAS before flying. The registrant must be at least 16 years old. The registration label must be affixed to a non-detachable part of the UA (e.g., top surface of the main body) and be clearly visible, not on batteries or propellers.

Operator Permit

  • Granted by CAAS to an organization or individual demonstrating the ability to operate UAs safely. Assessment includes organizational setup, safety management procedures, airworthiness, and personnel competency. Valid for up to one year.

Activity Permit (AP)

  • Granted for a single activity or block of repeated activities, considering location, type of operation, dates/times, operating altitude, and mitigation measures.
    • Class 1 AP: Required for non-recreational/non-educational purposes, or if a recreational UA is over 25kg, or an educational UA is over 7kg. Not valid without an Operator Permit.
    • Class 2 AP: Required for recreational or educational purposes (UA 25kg or below/7kg or below, respectively) if flying above 200ft AMSL or within a No-Fly Zone.

Unmanned Aircraft Pilot Licence (UAPL)

  • Minimum age: 16 years old.
  • Class A UAPL: Allows operation of any UA with a total mass not exceeding 25 kg.
  • Class B UAPL: Allows operation of any UA with a total mass exceeding 25 kg.
  • A proficiency check is required at least once every 4 years for UAPL holders.

UA Basic Training Certificate (UABT)

  • Required for operating drones between 1.5kg and 7kg for recreational or educational purposes. The minimum age is 16 years old.

No-Fly Zones

  • Areas where UA operation is restricted without requisite permits, including areas within 5km of aerodromes, Danger Areas, Protected Areas, Prohibited Areas, Restricted Areas, and Temporary Restricted Areas. OneMap (www.OneMap.gov.sg) is a useful tool for identifying these zones.

  • Invulnerability: "It won't happen to me."
  • Macho: "I can do it" (overestimating ability, underestimating situation).
  • Anti-authority: "Don't tell me."
  • Open-mindedness: This is not a hazardous attitude.

Purpose of Risk Assessment

  • To identify hazards specific to the activity and implement control/recovery measures to mitigate the risks.

SHELL Model

  • A conceptual framework used to analyze the interaction of different system components in aviation human factors: Software, Hardware, Environment, Liveware, Liveware (human-human interaction), with the central focus being the human participant (pilot).

Situational Awareness

  • The accurate perception and understanding of all factors and conditions within the four fundamental risk elements (PAVE), requiring a constant state of understanding what's happening and alertness to changes. Loss of situational awareness indicators include ambiguity, distraction, fixation, overload, complacency, and improper procedure.

Decision-Making Models

  • DECIDE Model: Detect, Estimate, Choose, Identify, Do, Evaluate.
  • 3P Model: Perceive, Process, Perform.

Loss of Control Link (Emergency Procedures)

  • If UA is not responding, no video feed, or a warning indicates RC lost:
    1. Move closer to the UA; attempt to regain control by adjusting the transmitter antenna.
    2. If the link is not regained after 8 seconds, the UA will automatically return to the take-off point.
    3. If the GPS signal is also lost, the UA will automatically land on the spot.

Loss of GPS Capabilities (Emergency Procedures)

  • If the UA drifts, does not maintain its hold position, or GPS signal strength is red:
    1. Fly the UA manually to avoid obstacles.
    2. Attempt to land the UA in a safe location as soon as practicable.
    3. If both GPS and the Vision Positioning System are faulty, switch to "manual" mode and attempt a safe landing.

Scanning Technique

  • Perform a series of short, regularly spaced eye movements that bring successive areas of the sky into the central (fovea) visual field. At night, scanning must be done more intensely and slowly.

Altimetry Terms

  • Above Ground Level (AGL): Height above the ground.
  • Above Mean Sea Level (AMSL): Height above the ground in relation to the average sea level datum.
  • At Take Off (ATO): Elevation at take-off, measured above mean sea level.

Weather Information

  • MyENV by NEA (app): Provides up-to-date national weather forecasts, warnings, rainfall, temperature, humidity, and wind information.
  • METAR (METeorological Terminal Aviation Routine): A weather-reporting format typically valid for 1 hour, providing wind direction/speed, visibility, cloud conditions, temperature/dewpoint, and atmospheric pressure.

Sensors

  • Accelerometer: Measures acceleration (useful for detecting vibrations).
  • Gyroscope: Measures orientation.
  • Barometer: Measures atmospheric pressure.
  • Magnetometer (Magnetic Compass): Measures location.

GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System)

  • A minimum of 4 satellites is required for a GNSS/GPS receiver to calculate its position (3 for latitude/longitude/height, 1 for clock synchronization).

Real-Time Kinematics (RTK)

  • A differential GNSS technology providing high-performance locating using a base station that broadcasts real-time data to users.

  • The conventional axes of motion are Longitudinal, Lateral, and Vertical. The Latitude axis is considered unconventional.
